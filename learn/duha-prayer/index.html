Duha Prayer: forenoon voluntary salah.

Editorial methodology

What is Duha prayer?

Duha prayer is a voluntary prayer offered in the forenoon, after the sun has risen and before Dhuhr. It is not one of the five obligatory prayers, but it is a beloved Nafl prayer that many Muslims keep as a morning habit.

Duha is especially useful for people who want a simple voluntary prayer outside the busy rhythm of the five daily prayers. Even two rak'ahs can become a steady act of gratitude and remembrance.

Duha belongs to the wider family of Nafl prayers. It is distinct from Tahajjud at night and from the regular Sunnah prayers around the five daily prayers.

Salatul Duha time: when is Duha prayed?

Duha begins after sunrise, once the sun is clearly up, and continues until before Dhuhr. Many teachers advise waiting roughly 15 to 20 minutes after sunrise before praying, while also avoiding the time just before the sun passes its zenith.

Because exact timings depend on location, date, and local scholarly guidance, follow your mosque calendar or trusted teacher for the safest practical window.

Wait

After sunrise

Allow the sun to rise clearly; many teachers use roughly 15 to 20 minutes as practical guidance.

Pray

Forenoon window

The broad Duha window continues through the morning.

Stop

Before Dhuhr

Avoid the period when the sun is at its zenith just before Dhuhr begins.

How many rak'ahs are in Duha?

Many Muslims pray Duha as two rak'ahs, and this is an easy place to begin. Other narrations and local teachings mention more, including four or eight rak'ahs. The best number for a beginner is often the one they can keep consistently.

Two rak'ahs

A simple, sustainable form and a common starting point for Duha.

Four or more

Some people add more rak'ahs when they have time, energy, and a teacher's guidance.

Consistency first

For voluntary prayer, a small habit done regularly is often easier to protect than a large plan done rarely.

How to pray Duha prayer

Duha is commonly prayed in sets of two rak'ahs. Make the intention in your heart, begin with the opening takbir, recite al-Fatihah and additional Quran, complete ruku and sujud, then pray the second rak'ah and finish with tashahhud and salam. Two rak'ahs are enough for a simple, sustainable beginning.

Keep the method familiar

No special surah is required by this guide. Pray with the normal form you have learned, and follow your madhhab or trusted teacher if your community teaches additional details.

Duha, Ishraq, and local names

Some communities use Ishraq for Duha prayed near the beginning of its time, soon after sunrise. Others use Ishraq and Duha as separate labels, or use names like Chasht in local languages. The worship is familiar across communities even when the vocabulary differs.

If your mosque or family uses a specific name, follow that local teaching. The important part is knowing the prayer's morning window and keeping it as voluntary worship, not treating it like an obligation.

Common questions

Is Duha prayer obligatory?

No. Duha is voluntary. The five daily prayers come first, and Duha is added as extra worship.

Can I pray Duha at work or school?

Yes, if you have a clean, appropriate place and enough time. A short two-rak'ah Duha can fit into many morning routines.

Can I pray Duha every day?

Yes. Many Muslims keep it as a regular habit, but it remains voluntary. Start gently and build consistency.

What if I miss Duha?

Do not let missing it discourage you. Protect the obligatory prayers and return to Duha the next day when you can.

Nafl Prayers: voluntary salah.

Nafl prayers are voluntary prayers beyond the required salah, giving Muslims more ways to worship, remember Allah, and build a steady prayer life.

At a glance

  • Meaning: voluntary prayers beyond the required daily salah.
  • Priority: protect the obligatory prayers first.
  • Examples: Duha, Tahajjud, mosque greeting, and prayer after wudu.
  • Note: avoid restricted times and follow local scholarly guidance.

What are Nafl prayers?

Nafl prayers are voluntary prayers offered beyond the required five daily prayers. They do not replace the obligations, but they help a Muslim add worship, seek reward, and grow closer to Allah through extra salah.

In everyday speech, people may use terms like nafl, voluntary, extra, or optional prayer. Some voluntary prayers have specific names and times, while others are more general two-rak'ah prayers prayed when allowed.

Obligatory first, then extra worship

The foundation is always the required prayer. Hadith emphasizes that the most beloved deeds are the obligations Allah has enjoined, then a servant continues drawing closer through extra deeds. Nafl prayers make the most sense when they support, not distract from, the five daily prayers.

A simple approach is to protect Fajr, Dhuhr, Asr, Maghrib, and Isha first. Then add one consistent voluntary prayer, such as two rak'ahs after wudu, Duha, or a short night prayer, instead of trying to do everything at once.

Common examples of Nafl prayers

Duha prayer

A voluntary forenoon prayer after sunrise and before Dhuhr, taught in many communities as a gentle daily habit.

Tahajjud or night prayer

Voluntary prayer at night, especially in the later part of the night, praised as an excellent prayer after the obligatory prayers.

Tahiyyat al-masjid

The two-rak'ah greeting of the mosque before sitting, when a person enters the masjid at a suitable time.

Prayer after wudu

Some Muslims pray two voluntary rak'ahs after making wudu, where time and circumstance allow.

When can Nafl prayers be prayed?

Some Nafl prayers have specific times, like Duha in the forenoon or night prayer after Isha. General voluntary prayer is broad, but not every moment is treated the same in Islamic law. Scholars discuss times when voluntary prayer is avoided, such as around sunrise, the sun's zenith, and sunset.

Details can differ by madhhab, circumstance, and the type of voluntary prayer. If you are learning, ask your imam which times to avoid and which voluntary prayers are best for your routine.

Praying Nafl at home

Many hadith encourage voluntary prayers at home, while the obligatory prayers and some special prayers have their own congregation guidance. Praying Nafl at home helps make the home a place of worship and can protect sincerity.

At the same time, real life has work, travel, mosque schedules, and community habits. If you pray some voluntary prayers in the mosque, keep the main principles in view: protect the obligations, avoid showing off, and follow sound local teaching.

Common questions

Are Nafl prayers required?

No. Nafl prayers are voluntary. The required daily prayers come first, then extra prayers can be added according to ability.

What is the difference between Sunnah and Nafl?

Sunnah prayers are connected to Prophetic practice and may have specific emphasis. Nafl is a broader category for voluntary prayer beyond the obligations.

How many rak'ahs should I pray?

Many voluntary prayers are prayed in sets of two rak'ahs, but named prayers can have specific guidance. Start small and ask a teacher about the prayer you want to add.

What is a good Nafl prayer for beginners?

Choose something sustainable, such as two rak'ahs of Duha, two after wudu, or a short night prayer once or twice a week.

Sources and further reading

  • Sahih al-Bukhari 731 - voluntary prayer at home except obligatory prayers.
  • Sahih al-Bukhari 444 - greeting the mosque with prayer before sitting.

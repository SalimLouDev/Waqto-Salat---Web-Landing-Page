Editorial methodology

What are Sunnah prayers?

Sunnah prayers are additional prayers connected to the practice of the Prophet Muhammad, peace and blessings be upon him. They are not the same category as the obligatory five daily prayers, but they are a beloved way to add worship, discipline, and remembrance to the day.

Some Sunnah prayers are tied to the daily prayers, while others are connected to special times or occasions. This page focuses mainly on the regular Sunnah prayers around Fajr, Dhuhr, Maghrib, and Isha, often called rawatib.

Start with the five obligatory prayers, then use this guide alongside the related pages for Witr and broader Nafl prayers.

Sunnah Mu'akkadah and non-emphasized Sunnah

Scholars often describe Sunnah prayers in levels. Sunnah Mu'akkadah means emphasized Sunnah: prayers the Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, is reported to have kept with special consistency. Non-emphasized Sunnah prayers are recommended too, but with a lighter level of emphasis.

The exact labels and details may differ between madhhabs. If you are following a teacher, mosque, or school of law, use their structure for what is emphasized, what is recommended, and how to arrange the prayers around your day.

The regular rawatib pattern

A widely taught pattern of regular Sunnah prayers is twelve rak'ahs in the day and night: four before Dhuhr, two after Dhuhr, two after Maghrib, two after Isha, and two before Fajr. Many Muslims learn this as a simple daily structure for Sunnah Mu'akkadah prayers.

Before Fajr

Two rak'ahs before the obligatory Fajr prayer are especially emphasized in many teachings.

Around Dhuhr

Four rak'ahs before Dhuhr and two after Dhuhr are part of the commonly taught twelve-rak'ah rawatib pattern.

After Maghrib and Isha

Two rak'ahs after Maghrib and two after Isha complete the regular pattern in many communities.

Other recommended prayers around salah

Beyond the regular rawatib, some communities also teach additional recommended prayers, such as four rak'ahs before Asr, extra prayers before Maghrib, or prayers connected to entering the mosque, making wudu, or the forenoon. These may be described as Sunnah, nafl, or voluntary depending on the context.

Keep the categories clear: the five daily prayers are required, the regular Sunnah prayers are strongly encouraged in many teachings, and other voluntary prayers add more worship without replacing the obligations.

Where should Sunnah prayers be prayed?

Many hadith encourage voluntary prayers at home, while obligatory prayers and some special prayers have their own communal practices. This helps a Muslim home remain a place of worship and keeps extra prayer sincere and steady.

In real life, people may pray Sunnah at the mosque because of work, travel, timing, or local practice. If you are unsure, follow your imam or a trusted teacher.

Common questions

Are Sunnah prayers obligatory?

No. They are not the same as the five obligatory prayers, though emphasized Sunnah prayers are treated with special importance by many scholars.

What are rawatib prayers?

Rawatib are the regular Sunnah prayers connected to the daily prayers, especially before Fajr, around Dhuhr, after Maghrib, and after Isha.

What if I miss a Sunnah prayer?

Do not let it make you give up. Keep the obligatory prayer first, then rebuild the Sunnah habit gently and ask your teacher about making up missed Sunnah prayers.

Do all madhhabs list the same Sunnah prayers?

No. The main pattern is widely recognized, but labels, counts, and emphasis can differ. Follow your madhhab or local scholarly guidance.

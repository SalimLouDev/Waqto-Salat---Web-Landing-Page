Witr Prayer: last prayer of the night.

Witr is the odd-numbered night prayer offered after Isha and before Fajr, often kept as the final prayer before sleep or before dawn.

At a glance

  • When: after Isha and before Fajr.
  • Meaning: an odd-numbered prayer that closes the night.
  • Common forms: one, three, five, seven, or other odd rak'ahs.
  • Note: ruling and Qunut details differ by madhhab.

Editorial methodology

What is Witr prayer?

Witr prayer is a night prayer with an odd number of rak'ahs. The word witr is connected to oddness, and the prayer is commonly used to close a Muslim's night worship after Isha, Tahajjud, or Taraweeh in Ramadan.

Witr is not one of the five daily prayers, but it has a special place in Muslim practice. Scholars differ in its legal classification: the Hanafi school commonly treats it as wajib, while many other Sunni scholars describe it as a strongly emphasized Sunnah. Follow your madhhab or trusted local scholar.

Witr often closes Tahajjud or other night prayer. It is also closely related to the wider guides on Sunnah prayers and Nafl prayers.

When is Witr prayed?

Witr is prayed after Isha and before Fajr. Hadith reports show the Prophet Muhammad, peace and blessings be upon him, prayed Witr at different times of the night, from after Isha until the last part of the night.

If you are confident you will wake before Fajr, many people prefer to leave Witr until the end of their night prayer. If you fear you may sleep through it, pray Witr before sleeping. The goal is to protect the prayer, not to make the schedule harder than you can maintain.

How many rak'ahs are in Witr?

Witr is prayed in an odd number of rak'ahs. A common form is three rak'ahs, while some people pray one rak'ah after night prayer, and others pray five, seven, or more as part of longer night worship. The details depend on the madhhab and the practice you have learned.

One rak'ah

Some narrations describe ending the night prayer with one rak'ah to make the total odd.

Three rak'ahs

Three rak'ahs are common in many communities, especially after Isha or Taraweeh.

Longer night prayer

Witr may also close a longer night prayer, with even rak'ahs prayed first and Witr at the end.

How to pray Witr prayer

Begin Witr after Isha and before Fajr with the intention to pray Witr. One-rak'ah and three-rak'ah forms are both common, and longer odd-numbered forms are also reported. Recite al-Fatihah and Quran in each rak'ah as you would in prayer, then complete the method taught in your madhhab.

1. Choose the form

Pray the odd number of rak'ahs taught by your trusted teacher, commonly one or three.

2. Follow your madhhab

The sitting pattern, salam, and how three rak'ahs are joined or separated differ.

3. Learn Qunut locally

Qunut wording and its place before or after ruku also differ between valid practices.

There is no single step sequence that represents every madhhab. This guide explains the shared outline without presenting one school-specific format as universal.

Qunut and local practice

Many Muslims recite a supplication called Qunut in Witr, but the timing, wording, and whether it is recited regularly differ between schools and communities. Some recite it before ruku, some after ruku, and some reserve forms of Qunut for particular situations.

Because Witr has real differences in practice, it is one of the prayers where following your imam is especially helpful. If you pray in congregation, keep the unity of the prayer and avoid turning valid differences into conflict.

Common questions

Should Witr be the last prayer of the night?

It is recommended in many teachings to make Witr the last prayer at night. If you might not wake later, pray it before sleep.

Can I pray after Witr?

If you already prayed Witr and later wake for extra prayer, ask your local scholar how your madhhab handles that situation. Many teachers say not to repeat Witr twice in one night.

Is Witr part of Isha?

No. Isha is one of the five obligatory daily prayers. Witr is a separate night prayer prayed after Isha and before Fajr.

What should a beginner do?

Learn the Witr format taught in your mosque or madhhab, keep it consistent, and ask a teacher about Qunut and whether to pray one or three rak'ahs.
